The Intro

The first point to make here, is that the game is designed to carry you through the levelling process via the Quest line. This is intentional, and not such a bad deal, so just go with it.  As a brand new account, there really are no shortcuts.  Once you've done it once, possibilities will open up to you for speeding up the development of additional characters.  But in the beginning, follow the quest line, and pay attention to the game mechanics they are trying to teach you.


It has also been pointed out that much of your levelling experience comes from actually killing mobs near your level.  If a level 80 character comes and helps you, what they really end up doing is still your experience points, so it's better (i.e. faster) if you do the quest levelling solo.

The intro quest line (the quests you must complete before you can interact with other users in the game) will take you to around Level 6, and does a pretty good job of giving you a chance to learn some of the basic mechanics of your character.



Money

That being said, some people will tell you that the quest line is also designed to give you all of the gear you will need.  True... and false.  Let me just tell it to you straight:  in this game, what you are going to need... is money.  In Neverwinter, there are MANY forms of currency, but your primary currency will be something called Astral Diamonds (commonly referred to as AD).  In the beginning, your primary method of earning AD is found here:



the Random: Leveling Queue.  You pull up this screen by pressing the K key, and ability to queue will start being available to you once you hit Level 10, which is easy to reach, again, if you just follow the questline from Sgt. Knox.  The reward for queuing and running this each day is 9,000 astral diamonds.  Running this will just become part of your daily routine.  The dungeons are made up of a random group of 5 people, and usually go quite fast.  You are paid in rough astral diamonds, which must then be refined.  You can do this by access your currency window, by pressing the I key to pull up your inventory, and then clicking on the currency tab.



Then just click on the Refine button.   The game puts a cap you probably won't have to worry about hitting for a long time of 100,000 rough astral diamonds refined into the spendable astral diamonds per day.  Also, if you don't manually refine your rough AD, the game will do it for you every night (up to the daily max). 

But once you have some money to spend, then what?


The Auction House

Affectionally referred to, in chat, as AH, the Auction House is where users buy and sell gear and items with each other.  You access the Auction House by clicking on this icon:



in the toolbar at the top of your screen.  The biggest deal about the auction house is learning how to search it. 



and probably the first thing you'll use it to search for is gear.

I'm just gonna use one example on this page, but the same principle applies to all of the equipment types, and really everything.  So let's pretend that I just hit level 20, and I wanted to go see what kind of options just opened up for me in the way of armor.  I would go to the auction house, click on Armor over on the left, then click the checkbox "Usable by me", and then I would add a level filter.  The level filter just means, what level does the user have to be to use the gear.  When I click on "Usable By Me", it automatically sets the highest level as mine, 20... but then to avoid all of the lower level junk, I put 15 into the first box.  So this means, show me all of the gear that requires user to be levels 15 - 20. 



First... notice the price of things.  In 1 dungeon run, I made 9000 AD... the things here are starting at 60.  :)  Click on the right paging arrow to review the list and look for your upper tolerance on price.  Don't spend too much.



I would personally recommend setting a cap of no more than 2000 per purchase while you are levelling.  Once you find the highest item you are willing to pay for, then just look for the cheapest buyout price for that particular item and pull the trigger.

In this case here, the quest line had me wearing



the Scale Armor of Assualt.  Now... I certainly could have soldiered on with this armor, I'm sure, and eventually got something better in the quest line... or... for 1300 AD in the auction house, I was able to pick up the Iron Hauberk +1, which gives me a 40 point defense boost over the quest line, a 100 point power boost, and a 400 hit point boost.  Plus, it's an additional 40 points in stat bonuses (the numbers under the line).  Paying attention to little details like this is a great habit to get into... and usually you can repeat this pattern of levelling up gear all the way to end game.  The baseline Undermountain gear, for example, is dirt cheap and typically better than your end gear from the previous mod.  Once you've pulled the trigger on buying something through the auction house, the item you bought is then sent to your mailbox, so you will need to familiar yourself with where those mailboxes are.  There is a convenient one looked just as you walk into Protector's Enclave.

One last point here is, if things seem above your price comfort level:



then just start taping down the minimum item level



In this particular case, the Adventurer's Sword Knot is what I currently have, I will probably go ahead and spend the extra and go over my budget to upgrade.  It's a judgement call, but always spend as little as possible to start with.  There are plenty of other things you will need to spend AD on.


Appearance

So... now that you know how to go about earning money and buying new gear, let's stop here very briefly just to talk about appearance.  As you move through the game, you will undoubtedly run across characters that have some very interesting looking costumes.  Most of the time, these are acheived with a process called Transmuting.  Basically what this means is that you can take a peice of gear, and you can make it appear to be a different peice of gear, while not changing any of it's bonus attributes.  It's strictly an appearance thing, but I personally think this is an important part of the game.  To do this, simple click on a peice of gear, and select "Change Appearance".



when the window comes up, you'll then have a dropdown that you use to choose which peice of equipment you want it to look like:



in this case, I'm going to change the appearance of my Scale Armor of Assault so that it looks like Marauders Garb.  The stats of Scale Armor of Assult will not change, just it's appearance.



for a small fee, of course.  In this case, it's 14 AD.  This price increases as the level of the items goes up, so by the time you hit end game, your transmutes will cost closer to 8000 AD, not a huge sum for you by that time.



Enchantments

So did you notice, in my previous screenshot, where some of the gear looked like it had a little checkbox on it?



Those boxes are enchantment slots... and enchantments in this game are a HUGE deal.  Enchantments are another way of adding stats to your character.  They can be attached to gear, but more importantly, they can be removed from that gear to (at a price in gold), so that as you upgrade gear, you can collect and keep your same enchantments.  This is because there is an entire levelly process just for enchantments.  In the beginning, I wouldn't worry about that part.  We acquire our enchantments the same way we acquired our cool new gear, through the Auction House.  Which enchantments you buy is a process you will need to work through, to be sure.  Balancing your stats is a topic I won't try to cover in detail on this page.  Just know that you have 3 types of enchantment slots:  Offensive slots, Defensive slots, and Utility slots.  Each type of enchantment will give different stats depending on which of these slots it is placed into.  For the purpose of this guide, for new players I am recommend that you use


Dark Enchantments for your Offensive slots.  This gives you armor penetration first, which is vitaly important.  Most new users come into the game thinking they need to stack up their Power stat.  Why armor penetration is more important to focus on first is because... you can have all the power in the world, but the hit won't count for anything if it doesn't make it past the monsters armor.  Armor penetration is what enables your hits to connect and deal damage.  In the end game, you will stack power (Radiant Enchantments) in all of your offensive slots, but in the beginning, put armor penatration there (affectionately referred to as ArmPen). 

For defense slots...


use Azure Enchantments (this gives a Defense boost)... and for the utility slots, those aren't nearly as important.  You will end up with a total of 4 utility slots you will need to fill.  In my case, I like to use



Wicked Enchantments for the utility slots, since that will give me a 10% increase on incoming healing plus a gold bonus.  Having gold is a very important part of the game too.  Many things require gold, including the ability to remove enchantments from your gear, so the more you can get there, the better.  With 4 Wicked Enchantments, I'll get about a 50% boost in my gold production.

One thing you will notice with Enchantments is that, as of MOD 16, Undermountain, the baseline enchantment is rank 8, so don't even bother refining anything lower than that unless it is specifically to finish a quest.  Rank 8s sell for around 1000 AD in the AH. 




Companions

The next order of business a new player needs to deal with is setting up a companion.  But don't let the singularity of that word fool you.  What you actually need to do is setup your companionS.  When you first start out:



You will have the opportunity to set a single runestone, a single peice of equipment, 1 companion bonus, 1 offense bonus, and 2 defense bonus.  These bonuses will in the bottom will be filled in with companions in your collection, and the more companions you get in a category, the better your overall  bonuses are going to be.  As you level up your character, additonal runestone slots and equipment slots will appear, as well as 2 additional bonus slots at the bottom (marked for level 30 and level 60).

There is a pet store in Protector's Enclave, and I believe the questline will take you through here to buy a pet.  However, I think these pets are mostly crap, so after completing the questline, don't waste more gold buying your pets here.  Instead, you guessed it, head on over to the AH.

There are a couple of important considerations when talking about buying pets.  Every pet can be taken through an upgrading process that can eventually bring it to a Legendary status, but this comes at a price.  These prices are:

The special deal with the archons is:  by getting the whole collection, they give set bonuses.  The value of their individual bonuses is increased because you equip the entire set.  To be clear, this means that you have to use the bonuses in one of your bonus slots, not just have the pet in your inventory.

With respect to which pet you should keep summoned?... in the example above, I would go with the Water Archon we bought.
